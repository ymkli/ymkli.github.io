Incoming Ph.D. student - Stanford Earth System Science

Wildfire smoke, ozone, and climate stress in a changing Earth system.

I am Yangmingkai Li, usually YMK. I work on air pollution and climate impacts with measurements, satellite products, machine learning, and high-resolution environmental datasets.

Before Stanford, I studied Environmental Science at Peking University. My recent projects ask how wildfire smoke changes ozone exposure, how hot-dry atmospheric patterns reduce ecosystem productivity, and how emissions respond to social disruption.

Research

Questions I keep coming back to

I am interested in environmental changes that are easy to feel but hard to attribute: smoke in the air, stalled ozone progress, compound heat and drought, vegetation response, and rebound in urban emissions.

01

When smoke arrives, what happens to ozone?

Wildfire smoke is usually discussed through PM2.5. I also study its effect on ground-level O3, exposure, and mortality burden.

02

How do hot-dry patterns leave a biological signal?

I use ecosystem productivity data to examine how compound climate stress and Rossby-wave-driven circulation reduce vegetation growth.

03

Can emissions inventories catch fast social changes?

High-resolution inventories make it possible to see how vehicle greenhouse gas emissions rebound after disruptions such as COVID-19.

Featured work

Fire smoke is becoming an ozone problem, not only a particle problem.

My Science Advances paper estimates smoke-driven ozone changes across the contiguous United States and links them to mortality burden. A related EarthArXiv preprint studies how wildfire smoke has offset decades of progress in reducing ozone exposure.
